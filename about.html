Meet the Founder

"I wasn't built for traditional education — but I was built for learning."

Cameron Covarrubias  ·  Founder, EdTerm

The Journey

A life spent inside
how wisdom actually moves.

At sixteen, I entered a four-year Japanese culinary apprenticeship that became the foundation for a life shaped by learning through experience, mentorship, and cultural immersion rather than traditional academic systems. Over the next two decades, that path carried me through kitchens, remote communities, mountains, fishing towns, and educational institutions across the world — and I kept encountering the same truth everywhere I went.

Age 16
Japan

The Apprenticeship

A four-year Japanese culinary apprenticeship — one of the world's oldest living systems of wisdom transfer. From traditional sushi counters to high-pressure hospitality environments, the most valuable knowledge was never written down. It lived in hands, in instinct, in the relationship between a master and an apprentice willing to watch, fail, and try again. This was my first education in how wisdom actually moves between human beings.

"The knowledge that mattered most couldn't be found in a textbook. It had to be earned."
Pacific
Mariana Islands, Western Pacific

Islands at the Edge of Modernity

Years living in the Mariana Islands brought me face to face with Chamorro and Palauan communities navigating the tension between cultural preservation and outside pressure. I witnessed firsthand how generational wisdom, identity, and survival are maintained entirely outside formal systems — through story, ceremony, relationship, and lived practice. When those systems erode, the knowledge doesn't just leave. It disappears.

"Wisdom without a vessel vanishes. Communities know this. We've forgotten it."
Alaska
Alaska — Birthplace of Heli-Ski & Coastal Communities

Where Wisdom Means Survival

I lived in Alaska for four years — based in the region recognized as the birthplace of the heli-ski industry — and spent that time moving between the extreme mountain environments that defined it and the coastal tourist towns that grew around it. In both worlds, what struck me wasn't the danger or the spectacle. It was how knowledge moved. The guides, the fishermen, the operators — their expertise wasn't certified, it was inherited. Passed person to person through seasons of proximity, failure, and hard-earned judgment. I watched that knowledge evolve in real time as the industry grew, and I watched what happened when the human chain of transmission broke. The lessons didn't transfer to brochures or training manuals. They disappeared.

"In extreme environments, you stop pretending that formal knowledge is enough."
Nepal
Nepal — Mountain Villages & Jungle Regions

Oral Tradition as Living Infrastructure

Time spent throughout Nepal's mountain villages and jungle regions deepened my understanding of human resilience, spirituality, and the ways communities preserve meaning through oral tradition and lived experience rather than institutional structure. In communities where formal literacy is limited and formal institutions are distant, wisdom is the infrastructure. It holds everything together. I began to understand that what we call "underdeveloped" communities often have more sophisticated systems for preserving experiential knowledge than anything we've built in the modern world.

"Every elder who dies without being heard takes an irreplaceable library with them."
China
China — Cities, Kitchens & Changing Communities

Modernization at Scale

Time spent throughout China added another dimension to that understanding. Moving through rapidly developing cities while engaging with traditional food culture, education systems, and everyday social structures revealed something I hadn't seen at this scale before: the tension between modernization and cultural continuity playing out in real time, across hundreds of millions of lives simultaneously. Generational knowledge, craftsmanship, and lived experience were disappearing not through neglect but through speed — societies evolving faster than the systems designed to preserve what made them human in the first place.

"When a society moves faster than its memory, the loss is invisible until it's permanent."
Global
Thailand · Qatar · International Academic Communities

Into Education Systems

That journey eventually led into educational systems and academic communities — engaging with researchers, universities, and organizations exploring the future of learning, AI, and human development. I arrived not as an academic but as someone who had spent twenty years studying how learning actually works in the world. What I found in those institutions confirmed what I'd suspected: the systems we've built are extraordinary at storing information and nearly useless at preserving the human wisdom behind it.

"The world is rapidly becoming better at storing information while losing the deeper human wisdom behind it."

What began as curiosity evolved into a realization: the world needed a system to capture what experience teaches — before it disappears.

EdTerm was built in response to that problem.

The Mission

Built for the learner
who didn't fit the model.

EdTerm exists to make wisdom searchable, mentorship accessible, and learning human again. Our Human Wisdom Database preserves the lived experiences of professionals around the world. Tero, our AI mentor, is trained on real human insight — offering guidance shaped by empathy, context, and reflection.

EdTerm was built for the person I used to be — the learner who didn't fit the model — and for the millions who feel the same. What began with curiosity and a suitcase is growing through partners, experts, universities, and learners who believe wisdom is a public good.
